| Activity      | Description                                                     |
| ------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Audit**     | Map every manual workflow, bottleneck, and owner-dependent task |
| **Design**    | Architect the automated system on paper before building         |

### 22. Founder's Story — Ayo

I began my professional career as an architect, drawing and designing real buildings, which was all I ever wanted. I was good at it, but when I left the firm I trained under, things fell apart. For months, I couldn't land a single client. My old Acer laptop (4GB RAM!) couldn't handle real architectural software, so I was forced to ditch professional tools like AutoCAD for Microsoft Paint. I still created good work, but whenever clients heard I'd used Paint, they'd lose faith. I was young, and I didn't even have my degree yet.

Out of necessity, I turned to software engineering. My plan was to earn enough from coding to fund my true dream of architecture. But as I learned to code, it started to feel like more than a backup plan. To my surprise, I found that software engineering and architecture had a lot in common: both are about problem-solving, designing robust systems that work in the real world, under pressure, and over time.

As I grew in tech, I started noticing the same pitfalls in startups and everyday businesses that plague construction projects with no blueprints: chaos, burnout, missed deadlines, wasted money and effort. # Discovery Call Framework

A 90-minute conversation to diagnose and know if we’re a fit.

---

## Before the call

A 5-minute prep ritual. Don't skip it.

- Research - Pull up their website, LinkedIn, and anything they sent. Know their team size, years in business, and rough industry before they say hello.

(Confirm research output during call)

---

## Opening (first 3 minutes)

As straight to the point as possible

> "Thanks for making time. I’m Ayo, I’m an Automations architect [insert tagline] I reached out to you because I think you’re a right fit for the kind of services I offer which means you have a business with a lot of processes which are mostly manual, this call is just to reinforce and affirm that. I want to understand how your business actually runs, where you're spending your time, where things break, what's working, etc. Then, I'll either sketch out what a fix looks like, or I'll tell you honestly that I'm not the right person. That being said, can you tell me about yourself and your business?"

---

## Question Bank 1 — The Week Question (10 minutes)

**The opener — pick one:**

- "Walk me through last week. What fires did you personally put out?"
- "Take me through your last 7 days. Where did your time actually go?"
- "What did you do yesterday that nobody else on your team could have done?"

**Follow-ups based on what they say:**

- "How often does that come up?"
- "Who else could do that if they had what they needed?"
- "What would have happened if you'd been on a flight?"
- "Is that a once-a-week thing or every day?"
- "When did this start being your job?"

**What you're listening for:**

The pattern of *owner-as-bottleneck*. They'll describe a week where their hands touched everything. Note specific tasks (not categories) — "I rebuilt the renewal report," not "I did some reporting." Specifics become scope later.

**Red flag to listen for:**

If their week sounds calm and structured, they're not in Box 2. Either they're already past you, or they're not yet at the pain threshold. Note it. Probe gently in the next section before deciding.

---

## Question Bank 2 — The Chase Map (15 minutes)

**The opener:**

- "Tell me where you chase. Employees, customers, leads, vendors — where are you the one sending the third follow-up?"
- How do you get updates from Employees
- How do you send payment reminders to customers

**Then probe each one they mention:**

_If they chase employees:_

- "What kind of updates? Status on client work, or something else?"
- "How do they currently let you know things are done?"
- "If you stopped asking on Monday, how long before things slip?"

_If they chase customers:_

- "Chase them for what? Payment, replies, scheduling?"
- "What's the trigger that makes you realize you need to chase?"
- "How many of these are you personally sending vs. someone on your team?"

_If they chase leads:_

- "When a lead comes in at 10pm on a Friday, what actually happens?"
- "What's the average time before someone gets back to them?"
- "How many do you think you've lost to slow response?"

_If they chase vendors:_

- "What kind of vendors? Suppliers, contractors, partners?"
- "What breaks if they're late?"

**The dollar-anchor question (slip this in once they've named 2–3 chase patterns):**

> "Rough guess — how many hours a week across you and your team go into chasing? Don't overthink it."

Whatever number they give, you've now got an hours-cost anchor for the scope conversation later.

**What you're listening for:**

Specific people, specific frequencies, specific consequences. "We chase a lot" is useless. "My account manager spends Mondays pulling the renewal list by hand" is gold.

---

## Question Bank 3 — Report Inventory (10 minutes)

**The opener:**

> "What reports do you build by hand? And who actually reads them?"

What is the revenue range? Then how do you know this

**Follow-ups:**

- "How often does that one get built?"
- "Where does the data live? One place, or are you pulling from three different tools?"
- "How long does it take you each time?"
- "What decision does that report drive?"
- "If you didn't build it next week, would anyone notice?"

**The killer question:**

> "What's the report you wish you had but don't, because building it manually would take forever?"

This one surfaces the *invisible* report — the data they want for decisions but can't justify the manual labor to build. It becomes the dashboard hook later.

**What you're listening for:**

Manual reporting is one of the easiest wins to scope. If they're building anything by hand more than once a month, that's a dashboard. Note the data sources — that tells you integration complexity.

---

## Question Bank 4 — Trying to understand current solutions they have in-place and how much it has helped (15 minutes)

**The opener:**

- "Walk me through the tools you use day to day. CRM, email, calendar, project management, anything else?"
- “Do you have any existing tool you use?”

**For each tool, ask:**

- "Who uses it?"
- "What do you use it *for*, specifically?"
- "Is it connected to anything else, or do you copy-paste between tools?"
- "When did you last try to make those two talk to each other?"

**The leftover questions:**

- "What's in spreadsheets that shouldn't be?"
- "Where does information live in someone's head instead of a system?"
- "Have you tried Zapier or Make? What happened?"

**What you're listening for:**

The handoffs. Anywhere a human is the bridge between two tools is a place a system should be. Also pay attention to the *Zapier story* — if they've tried and it broke, you have a ready reframe for objection §16. If they've never tried, the door is open.

**Red flag to listen for:**

"I just need a few Zapier zaps set up." That's a freelancer job. Note it. You'll either reframe the conversation toward systems, or refer them out at the end.

---

## Question Bank 5 — The Heaven Question (10 minutes)

This is the shift. You've spent 50 minutes in their hell. Now you flip it.

**The opener — pick one:**

- "If this all worked the way it should, what would your Tuesday look like?"
- "Picture six months from now, everything running. What's different about your day?"
- "What does your week look like when this is working?"

**Don't talk. Let them describe it.**

If they give you a vague answer ("less stressful, I guess"), pull on the thread:

- "What time would you close the laptop?"
- "What's the thing you'd stop checking on your phone?"
- "Who in your life notices first that something changed?"
- "What would you say yes to that you say no to now?"

**What you're listening for:**

Their language for Heaven (framework §6 and §15). Write down their exact words — "sleep through the weekend without checking Slack," "actually be present at dinner," "take two weeks off without it falling apart." Those words go in the audit report and the proposal. They sell better than yours do.

**Why this matters:**

People buy the future, not the build. The emotional anchor you create here is what carries them through the price conversation in section 7.

---

## Question Bank 6 — The Walk-Away Test (5 minutes)

This is the question most people skip. Don't skip it.

**The opener — pick one:**

- "Honest question. Is now actually the right time to fix this, or is this a 'someday' thing?"
- "What happens if you do nothing? What does this look like a year from now?"
- "What's stopping you from doing this six months ago?"

**Follow-ups depending on the answer:**

_If they say "now is the time":_

- "What changed recently?"
- "Who else needs to be part of the decision?"
- "What would have to be true for this to start in the next 30 days?"

_If they hesitate or say "soon":_

- "What would 'someday' need to look like before you actually move?"
- "Is there a deadline forcing this, or is this an internal pressure?"
- "What would make this not happen?"

**What you're doing:**

Killing weak leads early, so you don't spend two weeks chasing a maybe. Soft maybes become silent ghosts. You'd rather hear "not yet" now than wonder in three weeks.

**If they're not ready:**

Say so on the call. "Sounds like this is more of a Q3 conversation. Let's stay in touch, but I'd rather not put together a scope you won't act on." That sentence builds more trust than any proposal would.

---

## Scope Sketch + Next Step (25 minutes)

Either propose a Phase 1 or refer them out.

### If they're a fit

Walk through / brief diagnosis

> "Here's what I heard. You're spending around 25 hours a week on three things — chasing your AM for renewal status, rebuilding the same report on Mondays, and following up on leads that should have been auto-handled at 10pm Friday. Each one is fixable. Together they're probably costing you north of $200k a year between your time and the leads that go cold."

Then sketch Phase 1:

> "If we worked together, the first 8 weeks would look like this. Week 1, I map every workflow and hand you a Sanity Audit Report with a fixed quote. Week 2, we design the system on paper before building. Weeks 3–6, I build and integrate. Weeks 7–8, I train your team and hand it over. You own everything when I leave. Thirty days of post-launch support included."

Name the price range.

> "For the scope we just walked through, that's somewhere between $X and $Y. Final number lands after the audit. The audit itself is $Z and counts toward the build if you move forward."

_(Use actual numbers based on scope of work.)_

**Then stop talking. Ask:**

- "What's your reaction to that?"
- "What questions does that bring up?"
- "Where does that land for you?"

**Common objections will come up here.** Pull from §16 — don't argue, acknowledge the fear, reframe with the asset they're missing.

### Close the call

> “Thank you very much for taking time out of your already busy schedule to speak to me. I will send a recap of this call via mail then an agreement so we can start”

### If they're not a fit

> "Based on what we just walked through, I'm not the right person for this. You're at the stage where [reason — too early / too big / too DIY / pain isn't acute yet]. Here's who I'd talk to instead."

- Update your CRM or notes doc: fit status (green/yellow/red), Sanity Quadrant placement, scope sketch, follow-up date. — Take note of unanswered questions and empty fields
